Project Overview

Simpath reimagines how clinicians train by generating realistic, culturally diverse patient dialogues that help practitioners strengthen empathy, accuracy, and communication.

As product designer and AI research lead, I led the design and technical development of Simpath ‚Äî building workflows for persona generation, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), and feedback evaluation that improved training realism and bias transparency.

Problem

The U.S. faces a severe shortage of trained mental health providers, reducing access to care and increasing burnout among clinicians.

Traditional therapy training relies on costly, time-intensive supervision and limited exposure to diverse client experiences. As a result, practitioners often graduate without adequate preparation for real-world cross-cultural scenarios or confidence in their therapeutic communication.

Product Goal

Develop an AI-powered simulation tool that allows clinicians to practice patient interactions safely, receive feedback, and evaluate their performance.

The goal was to create an interactive, evidence-based training system that combines realistic AI personas with measurable outcomes for empathy, clinical accuracy, and ethical decision-making.

Methods

Simpath integrates large language models, prompt engineering, and feedback analysis to create authentic, bias-aware therapeutic scenarios.

Impact & Evaluation

Pilot evaluations showed that Simpath meaningfully strengthened training realism and produced measurable gains in clinician practice, while also revealing key limits and opportunities for improvement.

What we measured

‚óè

Conversational fidelity: AI-Generated Patient Personas successfully replicated conversational patterns present in real therapy transcripts, producing turn-taking, response structure, and domain-relevant phrasing that trainees found realistic.

‚óè

Task accuracy: Model performance varied by scenario complexity and clinical use case. Simpler, well-scoped prompts produced high-fidelity responses, while complex DSM-5 scenarios (comorbidities, nuanced affect) exposed gaps in emotional accuracy and clinical nuance.

‚óè

Training outcomes: Clinician testers reported clearer decision pipelines and improved confidence after repeated simulation sessions; structured evaluation showed improved decision-making trends in pilot tasks (see evaluation methods below).

‚óè

Fairness & bias mitigation: Iterative fine-tuning and targeted evaluation reduced several bias signals in generated responses; however, mitigation effects were scenario-dependent and require larger-scale testing to confirm robustness.


Evaluation methods

‚óè

Mixed-methods pilot: clinician ratings (qualitative realism, usability), blinded task assessments (decision accuracy), and automated fairness metrics across demographic slices.

‚óè

Human review loops: clinician annotators validated outputs and flagged failure modes used for targeted retraining and prompt adjustments.


Limitations & next steps

‚óè

Emotional nuance and complex clinical reasoning remain the largest failure modes; we plan expanded scenario coverage and human-in-the-loop adaptation to improve these dimensions.

‚óè

We are conducting larger-scale evaluations to quantify the effect sizes for accuracy and bias reductions and to validate transfer across different clinician populations and DSM-5 use cases.
